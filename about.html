Andrew Dessler is a climate scientist who studies both the science and politics of climate change. He is a Professor of Atmospheric Sciences and director of Texas A&M’s Texas Center for Extreme Weather

His scientific research revolves around climate feedbacks, in particular how water vapor and clouds amplify warming from carbon dioxide. He also studies the intersection of climate change and human society, including climate extremes and the stresses that a changing climate places on energy, water, infrastructure, and human systems.

During the last year of the Clinton Administration, he served as a Senior Policy Analyst in the White House Office of Science and Technology Policy. His books include The Science and Politics of Global Climate Change, written with Edward Parson; Introduction to Modern Climate Change; and The Chemistry and Physics of Stratospheric Ozone.

In 2011 he was named a Google Science Communication Fellow. In 2021 he received the American Geophysical Union’s Climate Communication Award. He is a Fellow of the American Association for the Advancement of Science and the American Geophysical Union.

He holds a B.A. in physics from Rice University and a Ph.D. in chemistry from Harvard University, and completed postdoctoral work at NASA’s Goddard Space Flight Center.
